You have a constitutional right to stay silent when questioned by any law enforcment official.

Only a judge can compell you to answer questions

Click here to learn more

Police cannot search your car without your consent, or probable cause to believe that criminal activity is taking place.

Refusal to consent to a search also cannot be used as probable cause.

Click here to learn more

The First Amendment grants U.S. citizens freedom of speech, press, petition, and assembly.

Some types of unprotected speech include libel, obscenity, and slander.

Click here to learn more

In 1962, the Supreme Court ruled that school sponsored prayer in public schools violates the First Ammendment, and is therefore illegal.

It's also illegal to begin school events with prayers, or to force students to attend religious clubs.

Click here to read more

States cannot ban the use of contraception, or abortion during the first trimester, as it violates an individual's right to privacy.

However, states can require pre-abortion counseling, waiting periods, and parental permission for minors.

Click here to read more

Anyone living in the U.S.- citizen or non-citizen- is guaranteed equal treatment under the law.

However, recent laws- like the Patriot Act, which reduces due process and privacy rights for immigrants- have infringed upon these rights.

Click here to see more

Anyone is allowed to take photos of clearly visible items and actions in public spaces- including government officials like police.

However, on private property, the owner may set rules about photography.

Click here to see more

Most U.S. citizens over the age of 18 are eligible to vote. It's illegal for states to institute literacy tests or overly strict Voter I.D. laws to prevent people from voting.

In order to vote, you must be registered in your state.

Click here to see more

Police officers cannot view or confiscate photos or videos without a warrant. Under no circumstance are they allowed to delete any photos or videos.

Warrants are also required before an officer can search your cell phone.

Click here to see more

Federal law prohibits discrimination in the workplace based on race, color, gender, pregnancy, sexual harassment, age, disability, national origin, religion or retaliation.

Employers are required to provide a workplace that is free of harrassment, and reasonably accomodates the religious practices of employees.

India Unger Harquail

is a high school senior from Montclair, New Jersey. Currently, she's enjoying building her coding knowledge and buisness acumen at Girls Who Code. India has been elected to student council for all four years of high school, and is a DECA Regional Champion. In addition to coding, she horseback rides, manages her project Acadium Scholar and teaches Java for the Montclair Code Community. India channels her passion for female empowerment through her internship at Hack It Back, an organization that runs media awareness and tech workshops for young girls

Serena Simpkins

is a rising junior at Montgomery High School in Montgomery, New Jersey. Serena works as a student delegate to the United Nations through the Working Group on Girls and is also a national representative for GSUSA. She will be a moderator for the annual event, the Day of the Girl Summit: Girls Speak Out held at the United Nations. Serena has been a Girl Scout for seven years, and is currently working on her gold award through the creation of a Human Trafficking club that focuses on spreading awareness and educating the community. Along with participating in peer leadership, playing lacrosse and the violin, Serena tutors other minorities in her community to ensure they are receiving the help that they need to excel in school. She hopes to incorporate my skills obtained from GWC into her passion for advocating against human trafficking and for female empowerment in the world.
